# Research Process — Detailed Guide

## 1. Defining Questions

Write down exactly what you're trying to learn before searching. Vague browsing wastes time.

- **Too vague:** "What are the current trends in AI regulation?"
- **Better:** "What new AI regulations have been proposed or enacted in the US and EU in the last 12 months? What do they require? Who do they affect?"

Break a complex research question into 2-4 sub-questions you can answer independently.

## 2. Query Strategy

Run at least 3 different queries per sub-question. Vary the angle:

| Angle | Purpose | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Direct | Broadest retrieval | "AI regulation 2025 2026" |
| Institutional | Official / authoritative sources | "EU AI Act implementation timeline site:europa.eu" |
| Critical | Counterpoints, known problems | "AI regulation criticism challenges opposition" |

After WebSearch: follow up with WebFetch on the 2-3 most promising results to get full article content.

## 3. Source Evaluation

See `source-evaluation.md` for the full rubric (authority / recency / bias / corroboration).

Quick filter before fetching: skip anything clearly marketing, undated, or without an identifiable author/publisher.

## 4. Extraction

Pull key findings from each source. Log:
- The specific claim
- Source (title, publisher, date, URL)
- Type: **fact** (verifiable data point), **analysis** (interpretation), **opinion** (stated view)

## 5. Synthesis

Combine findings:
- Lead with the consensus view
- Note where sources disagree and why
- Flag tentative conclusions with "limited evidence suggests…"
- Identify gaps: "I couldn't find reliable data on X" is more useful than guessing

## 6. Citation

Every claim in the synthesis traces back to a logged source. Include title, publisher, date, and URL.

## Key Principles

- **Triangulate.** Three independent sources are evidence.
- **Date everything.** Always include when each source was published.
- **Know what you don't know.** Flag gaps explicitly.

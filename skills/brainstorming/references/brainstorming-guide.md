# Brainstorming Guide

## Question Focus Areas

- **Goal:** What are we trying to accomplish?
- **Audience:** Who will see/use the output?
- **Format:** What form should the deliverable take?
- **Sources:** What information or tools should we use?
- **Success criteria:** What does "done" look like?
- **Constraints:** Deadline, length, tone, budget, sensitivity?

## Approach Proposal

Once you understand the scope, propose 2-3 approaches with trade-offs:
- Example: (A) Comprehensive report with primary web research (thorough, takes longer). (B) Executive summary from the information already shared (fast, less complete). (C) Data-driven analysis with visualizations (best for quantitative topics).

## Key Principles

- **One question at a time.** Don't overwhelm with a wall of questions.
- **Multiple choice when possible.** "Should the report be (A) a 2-page executive summary, (B) a detailed 10-page analysis, or (C) a slide deck?" is easier to answer than "What format do you want?"
- **YAGNI.** If the human says "just a quick email," don't propose a multi-phase research project. Scale the methodology to the task.

## Anti-Pattern: Premature Execution

If you catch yourself writing the deliverable before the brief is done, stop. The brief is the contract. "I assumed you wanted a formal report" is the knowledge-work equivalent of "I assumed you wanted a microservices architecture."

## Anti-Pattern: Treating Form Responses as Approval

When a form or rich upfront context returns a lot of detail — priorities listed, files referenced, concerns specified — it can feel like permission to execute. It isn't. That context is the *raw material* for the brief. The brief still needs to be written, shown to the human, and confirmed before any work starts.

Fast-tracking means you can skip the question-asking phase (because the context is already there). It does not mean you skip showing the brief and waiting for a "yes."

**Wrong:** Form returns data → write brief → read files and start executing.  
**Right:** Form returns data → write brief → show brief → wait for approval → execute.
